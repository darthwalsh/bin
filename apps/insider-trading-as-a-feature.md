#ai-slop 
Current insider trading law treats material nonpublic information as something to _quarantine_ — lock out the C-suite until the information goes public, then let the market reprice. It works reasonably well for corporate executives, but it collapses entirely for politicians and government officials with legitimate access to market-moving information (Fed decisions, drug approvals, war and peace). We either ban them from trading entirely, or we accept the asymmetry — there's no clean middle ground in the current framework.

**A different model: disclosure-then-delay, not restriction.** Instead of banning trades on material nonpublic information, require the _bid itself_ to become public the instant it's placed — a short lockout (seconds, not hours) before it executes. Anyone can trade alongside it at the same moment; a trillion dollar index found *could* blindly copy the C-suite's or a politician's trades if it wants, or feed the signal into an algorithm as one more data point. The information advantage doesn't get erased by prohibition — it gets erased by giving everyone the same starting gun.

This doesn't fully close the loop, though. If insiders know a wall of capital reflexively follows their trades, they can manufacture moves that have nothing to do with real information — buy garbage, let the copycat money inflate it, profit from the _signal_ rather than the underlying fact. Disclosure-then-delay solves information asymmetry; it doesn't solve reputation-as-a-weapon. That second problem — where your own credibility becomes a market-moving instrument — might be the harder one, and it's probably a question for people who actually work in market microstructure rather than armchair speculation.
